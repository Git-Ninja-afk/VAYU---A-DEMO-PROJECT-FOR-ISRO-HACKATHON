Just like in your file, this is pure documentation at the top.
It tells anyone reading it that this script takes climate scenarios (like less rain or more heat) and translates 
them into real-world impacts for Central India.

These are our baseline assumptions for the math ahead. 
It says, "Right now, Central India gets about 18 heatwave days a year. 
For every 1 degree Celsius the Earth warms, add 6 more days."

</Function 1: drought_risk — The IMD Dictionary/>

def drought_risk(departure_pct):
    if departure_pct >= 20:
        return "Excess (flood watch)", "high", "surplus rainfall, runoff/flood risk"
    elif departure_pct > -20: ...
What it does:
This is a referee. You hand it a percentage (how much the rain changed), 
and it blows the whistle to tell you the official Indian Meteorological Department (IMD) category.

\Different cases/:
+20% or more? Excess rain (floods).

Between -20% and +20%? Normal.

Below -20%? Drought.

Below -60%? Severe drought.
It returns three things: a title, a severity level, and a quick note on the impact.

What it does:
This is the heart of the "what-if" machine.

new_rain: It takes the normal annual rain and multiplies it by your change. 
(If rain_change_pct is -30, it calculates 70% of the normal rain).

drought_risk: It calls the referee function we just talked about to get the text labels.

heatwave: It does the math on the temperature (18 days + your warming * 6).

return: It bundles all this math into a neat dictionary (a package of data) and hands it back.

</Function 3: main — The Conductor/>

def main():
    ds = xr.open_dataset(PROC)
    daily = ds["rain"].mean(dim=["lat", "lon"]).to_series()
What it does:
It opens your processed Central India data file. 
The mean(dim=["lat", "lon"]) part smoothes the entire map into a single average number for each day. 
It answers the question: "On average, how much did it rain across all of Central India today?"

Command:
    annual_totals = daily.groupby(daily.index.year).sum()
    annual_rain = float(annual_totals.mean())
What it does:
It groups the daily data by year (2005, 2006, etc.) and sums it up to get total yearly rainfall. 
Then it averages those totals together to find the "Normal" baseline for Central India.
 (It does the exact same thing for the Monsoon months of Jun-Sep right below this).
