5) The error functions (<<<IMPORTANT>>>)
def rmse(pred, true):
    return float(np.sqrt(np.nanmean((pred - true) ** 2)))

This computes RMSE — Root Mean Squared Error, this is actually the main thing which will determine the score of out ai bot 

The actual process of how it computes the score : 
pred - true — the miss on each day (predicted minus actual figure).
** 2 — square each miss (makes them all positive, and punishes big misses harder).
np.nanmean(...) — average those squared misses (the nan version ignores NaN cells so blanks don't break it).
np.sqrt(...) — square-root it, to bring the units back to mm/day.

So RMSE = "typical size of a miss, in mm." Lower = better.
Eg : 
A 2mm RMSE means predictions are usually ~2mm off.

As it is so important aspect of our whole program let's understand it once again : 


----> So first what is RSME ::
RMSE is the most important number in your whole project
(it's how you'll prove your AI is good), so it's worth really getting

----> Now The setup: what are we even measuring? ::
How wrong were you, on average? That's all RMSE answers. 
To compute it, we need two lists:

true — what actually happened (the real rainfall).
pred — what you predicted.
Let's take a eg : A data of 3 days 
Day   pred(you guessed)    true(reality)
1        10                   12
2        5                     5
3        8                    20 

Now let's see how it is computed through the formula np.sqrt(np.nanmean((pred - true) ** 2))

Step 1: pred - true → the "miss" each day
We will subtract reality from your guess, day by day. 
This is your error each day — how far off you were.

Day        pred − true             =miss
1            10-12                  -2
2             5-5                    0
3             8-20                  -12

Do you Notice a problem: some misses are negative and some will be positive (too high). 
If we just averaged these as it is, a −5 and a +5 would cancel out to 0 — making a terrible predictor look perfect! 
We need to fix that. That's the next step.


Step 2: ** 2 → square each miss
** 2 means "raise to the power 2," i.e. multiply each number by itself.

Day      miss      (miss)²
1         -2         4
2         0          0
3         -12        144

Squaring does two magic things:

A)Kills the negatives. A negative times a negative is positive. Now every miss is positive, 
so they can't cancel each other out.

B) Punishes big misses HARD. For Day 1 missed by 2 → it became 4. Day 3 missed by 12 → it became 144. Day 3's miss was 6× bigger, 
but after squaring it's 36× bigger (144 vs 4). So rather than showing slight errors the RSME points out where you go off and tells 
you about your model's accuracy .

C) np.nanmean(...) → average them
Now average those squared misses to get one summary number.
(4 + 0 + 144) / 3 = 148 / 3 = 49.33
So the mean squared error is 49.33. 
One number summarizing "how wrong were we overall."
---> Now, Why nanmean and not just mean? 
Remember NaN = "no data here" (those blank ocean/edge cells) . A normal average chokes on NaN — if even one value is NaN, plain .mean() returns NaN and ruins everything. 
The nan-version (nanmean) politely skips the NaNs and averages only the real numbers , foolproofing the entire process . 

D) Now np.sqrt(...) → square-root it back
we squared everything, which also squared the units. Our rainfall is in mm, 
but 49.33 is in "mm-squared" . To undo the squaring and get back to plain mm, we take the square root:
√49.33 = 7.02
RMSE = 7.02 mm.
That's the final answer, and now it's readable: "on a typical day, this predictor is about 7mm off."

I think now it's much more clearer .

We report both RSME and MAE because together they paint a fuller picture.

11) Baseline 1: Persistence ("tomorrow = today")
persist_pred = test.shift(1)
Persistence = "tomorrow = today." So to predict any day, we just use yesterday's actual value. 
The challenge in code: how do we line up "yesterday's value" next to "today"? 
That's the one job .shift(1) does.
What .shift(1) actually does
.shift(1) takes a column of values and slides every value down by one row. Everything moves down one step; the top gets a blank (NaN).

your test data (actual rainfall) looks like this:
Date         test (actual rain)
1 jan 2021     8
2 jan 2021     3
3 jan 2021     12
4 jan 2021     5
5 jan 2021     9

Now apply test.shift(1) — every value drops down one row:
Date         test.shift(1)
1 jan 2021     NaN      ← nothing above to slide down, so blank
2 jan 2021      8        ← slid down from Jan 1
3 jan 2021      3        ← slid down from Jan 2
4 jan 2021     12       ← slid down from Jan 3
5 jan 2021      5        ← slid down from Jan 4

merging the 2 :

Date         test (actual)   persist_pred (shifted)
2021-01-02      3                  8       ← Jan 1's value!
2021-01-03      12                 3       ← Jan 2's value!
2021-01-04      5                  12      ← Jan 3's value!
2021-01-05      9                  5       ← Jan 4's value!
    
So what we are doing here On each row, persist_pred now holds the previous day's actual rainfall. That's exactly the persistence prediction: 
"my guess for today = what actually happened yesterday."
So on Jan 2, our prediction is 8 (yesterday's real value), and reality was 3. On Jan 3, prediction is 3 (yesterday), reality was 12. 
The shift automatically lined up yesterday's value next to today, for every single day at once — no loop needed.
The .iloc[0] = train.iloc[-1] just patches the one blank it leaves at the very top.

12) Baseline 2: Climatology ("the seasonal normal")
doy_avg = train.groupby(train.index.dayofyear).mean()

dayofyear turns each date into a number 1–365 (Jan 1 = 1, Dec 31 = 365). 
groupby(...).mean() then averages all the training years together for each day-of-year.
So doy_avg[196] = "the average rainfall on day 196 across 2005–2020." 
This is the "seasonal normal" — built only from training data
